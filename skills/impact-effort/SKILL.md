# Skill — Impact-Effort Matrix (Strategic Alignment Edition)

The impact-effort matrix is a rapid prioritization instrument that maps initiatives
on two axes — expected customer or business impact versus estimated delivery effort —
producing four quadrants: Quick Wins (high impact, low effort), Major Bets (high
impact, high effort), Fill-ins (low impact, low effort), and Deprioritized (low
impact, high effort). In isolation the matrix optimizes for tractability, not
strategic value — its central failure mode under Doctrine #1. This skill closes
that gap with three mandatory enhancements: (1) a BCG Growth-Share Matrix portfolio
overlay (Bruce Henderson, BCG Henderson Institute, 1970) to determine which product
lines receive prioritization energy before individual feature scoring begins; (2) a
Roland Berger Design-to-Value (DtV) Workshop calibration step to ground effort scores
in cross-functional teardown data rather than delivery-team estimates; and (3) a
mandatory strategic-alignment gate — every item in the high-impact/low-effort quadrant
that does not ladder toward the current strategic objective is deprioritized regardless
of matrix position. McKinsey wave sequencing then converts the ranked matrix into a
phased delivery plan with explicit ROI checkpoints.

> **Doctrine:** Every external benchmark, framework attribution, or quantitative claim
> must cite a real source (author / firm / year). Never cite "50% of features never
> used" — origin is untraceable. Never cite NPS as a sole growth driver, story-points
> as a productivity signal, or the 40% PMF threshold as a universal law. The BCG
> overlay must precede individual feature scoring. The strategic-alignment gate must
> precede wave assignment. Unknown data → "unknown [assumption — verify]".

## Procedure

1. **Run the BCG portfolio overlay.** Before scoring any initiative, classify each
   product line or business unit using the BCG Growth-Share Matrix (Bruce Henderson,
   BCG Henderson Institute, 1970): Stars (high market share, high growth — invest to
   lead), Cash Cows (high share, low growth — harvest margin, fund Stars), Question
   Marks (low share, high growth — run a small bet first, validate before scaling),
   Dogs (low share, low growth — exit or divest). Only Stars and Cash Cows receive
   full impact-effort prioritization energy. Question Marks receive a capped
   exploration budget. Dogs are excluded from the matrix entirely. This step ensures
   the matrix's output is portfolio-coherent, not locally optimized.

2. **Calibrate effort scores with Roland Berger Design-to-Value (DtV).** Convene a
   cross-functional teardown workshop following Roland Berger's DtV methodology
   before assigning effort scores. Decompose each candidate initiative into its
   cost-of-complexity components: features contributing to margin are distinguished
   from those adding only variant complexity. Benchmark: Roland Berger DtV
   engagements document an average 45% reduction in variant count without revenue
   loss, confirming that perceived effort often reflects accumulated complexity cost
   rather than genuine customer value (Roland Berger, Design-to-Value, 2018).
   Effort scores revised after this teardown are more reliable than estimates
   generated by the delivery team in isolation.

3. **Define the current strategic objective explicitly.** State the single governing
   strategic objective for this planning cycle — the OKR, strategic theme, or
   board-level commitment that all prioritization must serve. This is the alignment
   gate anchor. If the objective is ambiguous or contested, stop and resolve it with
   the executive sponsor before proceeding. The alignment gate cannot function without
   a clear and agreed-upon target.

4. **Score each initiative on impact and effort.** Using a 1–10 scale, score every
   in-scope initiative (within BCG-selected product lines) on: Impact — expected
   uplift on the governing outcome metric (revenue, retention, activation rate —
   concrete and measurable, not proxy); Effort — time, headcount, and complexity
   cost after DtV calibration. Document scoring assumptions explicitly. Do not use
   story-points as an effort proxy — they measure scope agreement, not work volume.

5. **Apply the strategic alignment gate (Doctrine #1).** For each initiative in the
   high-impact/low-effort quadrant (Quick Wins), add a third binary axis: does this
   initiative ladder toward the current strategic objective? Items answering No are
   deprioritized regardless of their impact and effort scores. This gate directly
   closes the matrix's primary failure mode — prioritizing tractable items that do
   not advance the strategy. Record all gated-out items with rationale so they can
   be revisited when the strategic objective rotates in the next planning cycle.

6. **Assign wave sequence using McKinsey wave logic.** Sort surviving initiatives into
   delivery waves following McKinsey wave sequencing principles (McKinsey & Company,
   wave-based mobilization, 2012): Wave 1 (Weeks 1–12) — high-impact/low-effort
   initiatives that passed the alignment gate, generating quick wins with visible ROI
   checkpoints at week 6 and week 12; Waves 2–3 (Weeks 13–36+) — high-impact/
   high-effort major bets, requiring phased investment approval and staged go/no-go
   gates; Eliminated unconditionally — all low-impact/high-effort items, no parking
   lot, no negotiation; Fill-ins (low-impact/low-effort) — optional capacity fillers
   assigned only after Waves 1–3 are fully resourced.

7. **Document and hand off.** Produce the output block below. Hand off the ranked
   wave list to soldier-portfolio-wave-planner (O3 — McKinsey Wave-Based
   Mobilization, Standard grade) to convert it into a staged investment portfolio
   with explicit commitment boundaries per wave. Do not author execution plans,
   sprint structures, or delivery roadmaps here — that is the downstream soldier's
   scope.
